Understanding Entropy

Entropy... This terrifying, mystical concept. I will try my best to explain it, but don't fret if you don't entirely understand it, as I definitely still don't (and I got an A in my thermo class). However, I understand it enough to point out flaws in CCP.

Entropy is commonly defined as a measure of disorder in a system. It is a property, which means that it is measureable and can describe the current state of a system. In thermodynamics, it contains the units [Joules]/[Kelvin], or just [Energy]/[Temperature].

The Second Law of Thermodynamics

I recently took a classic thermal-fluids class at MIT called 2.005, which provided me with a solid foundation in basic thermodynamics laws. Within just the first few lectures, we learned about the second law of thermodynamics, which is

$$ \Delta S = S_{trans} + S_{gen} ,$$

where $ \Delta S $ is the change in entropy of a system, $S_{trans}$ is the entropy transfered into a system, and $S_{gen}$ is the entropy generated in a system.

A system can be literally whatever you want in thermodynamics. However, to analyze carbon capture technology, let us define our system to be the atmosphere of the entire world.

A key point to understand from the second law of thermodynamics is that $ S_{gen}$ can only be positive, and entropy is always generated as long as you have friction, viscous effects in a fluid (including the air), any sort of temperature differential (basically, any two connected sources with different temperatures), or any other reversibility, which is basically impossible to entirely remove.
